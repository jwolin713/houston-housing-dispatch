# Voice Guide Draft: Houston Housing Dispatch

**Status:** Draft for review
**Date:** 2026-03-01
**Goal:** Sharper insider voice inspired by Curbed, Brownstoner, Alison Cook, Erica Chen

---

## Core Philosophy

> "Interesting houses that can become long-lasting homes"

**Not:** Investment returns, flipping potential, or deals
**Yes:** Character, quality, fit, and what it's actually like to live there

**Voice:** Knowledgeable Houston insider who's watched neighborhoods evolve—like getting listings texted from a friend who happens to know every street in the Inner Loop.

## Listing Description Examples (12 new)

### Heights / Historic

**1. Heights bungalow, 1925**
> The kind of Heights bungalow that used to go for under $400K—back when that was a lot for the neighborhood. This one's been updated without erasing its history: original pine floors sanded back to life, period-appropriate trim work, and a kitchen that acknowledges microwaves exist. The lot's tight but the mature pecan in back provides actual shade, not just landscaping.

**2. Heights Victorian, 1910**
> One of the oldest houses in the Heights still standing, and it shows in the best ways—12-foot ceilings, transom windows, the kind of woodwork that would cost six figures to reproduce today. Previous owner ran it as an Airbnb, so everything's been permitted and updated behind the walls. The wraparound porch alone is worth the price of admission.

### Montrose / Museum District

**3. Montrose townhome, 2018**
> Three-story Montrose townhome on the quiet side of Westheimer, which means you can walk to Hugo's for Sunday brunch without crossing six lanes of traffic. The rooftop deck actually gets used here—downtown views, room for a grill, and enough space that your guests aren't standing in single file. Builder included the elevator shaft even if you don't need it yet.

**4. Museum District fourplex conversion, 1940s**
> Former fourplex converted to a single-family in the '90s, which means you're getting 3,400 square feet for what one-story ranches cost nearby. The bones are solid—poured concrete foundation, real plaster walls—and the layout's weird in a good way: four separate entrances if you're antisocial, or one big house if you're not. Two blocks from the Menil, if that matters.

### Midtown / EaDo

**5. EaDo warehouse conversion, 2020**
> One of the first loft conversions in EaDo before the neighborhood got loud about being a neighborhood. The 16-foot ceilings and original brick aren't affected—that's the whole point. What you're paying for is walking distance to 8th Wonder and not hearing your neighbor's Peloton through the walls. Two parking spots included, which is rare for the area.

**6. Midtown cottage, 1930s**
> A legitimate 1930s cottage in Midtown that somehow survived the townhome rush. It's tiny—950 square feet, two bedrooms, one bath—but the lot is the story. Oversized for the block, mature oaks, and enough room for an ADU if the city ever makes permitting reasonable. Current zoning allows it; current process does not.

### West U / Bellaire / Meyerland

**7. West U traditional, 1960s**
> West U finally has something under $1.5M, and here's the catch: it needs everything. Roof, HVAC, kitchen, baths—the full gut job. But the bones are there: 8,000 square foot lot on a quiet street, original hardwoods that could come back to life, and room for a pool without sacrificing the entire backyard. Someone's going to make this house incredible; might as well be you.

**8. Bellaire mid-century, 1958**
> One of those Bellaire ranches that real estate Twitter argues about—is it worth saving, or is the lot the value? We'd argue this one's worth saving. Original brick fireplace, clerestory windows in the living room, the kind of open plan that mid-century architects actually designed instead of HGTV'd into existence. Bonus: the sellers kept the terrazzo floors.

**9. Meyerland, post-Harvey rebuild**
> Meyerland house that flooded in Harvey and was rebuilt properly—not just dried out and flipped. We checked: new foundation, new electrical, new HVAC, elevated two feet above the old slab. The neighborhood's still recovering, but the house itself is functionally new construction at half the price per square foot.

### River Oaks / Tanglewood

**10. River Oaks adjacent (Southampton)**
> Southampton technically, but the kind of Southampton lot that backs up to actual River Oaks. The house is a 1950s ranch that someone's already gutted to the studs—you're buying a shell on a great lot. Bring your architect. The live oak in the front yard is older than the neighborhood.

### Oak Forest / Garden Oaks

**11. Garden Oaks, double lot**
> Double lot in Garden Oaks—actual double lot, two tax IDs, not just a big yard that could maybe be subdivided. The house is fine: three bed, two bath, updated enough to live in while you plan what's next. But the lot is the play here. Sell half, build new, or just enjoy having neighbors who can't see into your windows.

**12. Oak Forest A-frame, 1962**
> The A-frame that Oak Forest lifers have been watching for years. It hits the market every decade or so, and each time someone says they're going to restore it properly—and then doesn't. This could be the time. Vaulted ceilings, original stone fireplace, a floor plan that doesn't make sense until you live in it. Needs work, but the weird houses are the ones worth keeping.

## Intro Paragraph Examples (6 new)

**1. Neighborhood diversity lead**
> From a Heights Victorian that predates most of the neighborhood to new EaDo lofts with downtown views, this week's mix actually feels like Houston—diverse, spread out, and hard to categorize. Montrose delivers the walkability, West U delivers the schools, and Garden Oaks delivers a double lot that's been waiting for the right buyer.

**2. Price range lead**
> The spread this week runs from a $300K Meyerland fixer to a River Oaks-adjacent lot with an asking price that assumes you'll tear down what's there. Most of the action is in the $500K-$800K range, where updated bungalows and honest mid-centuries compete for buyers who missed the market five years ago.

**3. Weather/timing lead**
> Three weeks of rain meant fewer open houses and more price reductions. If you've been waiting for negotiating room, this is the week to look. The Heights has options at every price point, and Montrose finally has something that's not a skinny lot townhome.

**4. Thematic lead (character homes)**
> If you've been doom-scrolling through gray paint and LVP flooring, this week's a palate cleanser. Original hardwoods in Bellaire, transom windows in the Heights, and a Garden Oaks A-frame that's been teasing the neighborhood for years. Not everything needs updating—sometimes it just needs someone who gets it.

**5. Value-focused lead**
> Three houses this week that are priced like projects but move-in ready if your standards are flexible. The Meyerland post-Harvey rebuild is functionally new; the West U ranch needs everything but rewards the work; and the Midtown cottage is just small, not broken.

**6. Insider knowledge lead**
> The house that Oak Forest neighbors have been watching since the '90s finally hit the market. We've got that, plus a Museum District fourplex conversion with more square footage than you'd guess, and a Heights bungalow that's proof you don't need to gut everything to make it work.

## Phrases That Work (Insider Vocabulary)

### Architecture & Structure
- "the kind of [feature] that would cost six figures to reproduce"
- "bones are solid"
- "original [material] that could come back to life"
- "properly permitted"
- "functionally new construction"
- "converted without erasing its history"
- "the weird houses are the ones worth keeping"

### Neighborhood Context
- "the quiet side of [street]"
- "before the neighborhood got loud about being a neighborhood"
- "backs up to actual [upscale area]"
- "walking distance to [specific restaurant/landmark]"
- "[Neighborhood] lifers have been watching this one"
- "survived the townhome rush"

### Practical Observations
- "the lot is the play here"
- "bring your architect"
- "priced for the lot, not the finishes"
- "needs everything, but the bones are there"
- "acknowledges [modern thing] exists"
- "two parking spots included, which is rare"

### Value Commentary (use sparingly)
- "what [property type] used to cost before [neighborhood] got expensive"
- "half the price per square foot"
- "rewards the work"
- "the catch is..."

### Honest Assessments
- "it's tiny, but..."
- "the house is fine; the lot is the story"
- "layout's weird in a good way"
- "not broken, just small"
